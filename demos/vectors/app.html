Vector addition is used to sum together multiple vectors of the same dimension. The resultant vector is defined by the sum of the individual components of each vector. For example, if we have two vectors: $$\vec{u}=2\boldsymbol{\hat{\imath}}+3\boldsymbol{\hat{\jmath}}$$ $$\vec{v}=4\boldsymbol{\hat{\imath}}+7\boldsymbol{\hat{\jmath}}$$ then their sum is: $$\vec{u}+\vec{v}=(2+4)\boldsymbol{\hat{\imath}}+(3+7)\boldsymbol{\hat{\jmath}}=6\boldsymbol{\hat{\imath}}+10\boldsymbol{\hat{\jmath}}$$ In matrix notation, this may written in either of these two ways: $$\vec{u}+\vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix}2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix}4 \\ 7 \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}6 \\ 10 \end{bmatrix}$$ $$\vec{u}+\vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix}2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix}4 & 7 \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}6 & 10 \end{bmatrix}$$ And in ordered-set notation: $$\vec{u}+\vec{v}=\langle2, 3\rangle+\langle4, 7\rangle=\langle6, 10\rangle$$ Note that the same method of adding individual components cannot be used for vectors written in polar, cylindrical, or spherical coordinates: $$\vec{u}+\vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix}3.6 \\\angle56° \end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix}8.1 \\ \angle60° \end{bmatrix}\ne\begin{bmatrix}11.7 \\ \angle116° \end{bmatrix}$$ Instead, they must first be converted to cartesian coordinates then summed.

Scalar multiplication of vectors is used to multiply the magnitude of vectors by a scalar quantity without altering the direction of the vector. For example, given the 3-dimensional vector below:$$\vec{u}=2\boldsymbol{\hat{\imath}}+4\boldsymbol{\hat{\jmath}}+1\boldsymbol{\hat{k}}$$ Performing a scalar multiplication by a factor of 3 would result in the following: $$3\vec{u}=(3\cdot2)\boldsymbol{\hat{\imath}}+(3\cdot4)\boldsymbol{\hat{\jmath}}+(3\cdot1)\boldsymbol{\hat{k}}=6\boldsymbol{\hat{\imath}}+12\boldsymbol{\hat{\jmath}}+3\boldsymbol{\hat{k}}$$ In matrix notation, this may be written in either of these two ways: $$3\vec{u}=3\begin{bmatrix}2 \\ 4 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}6 \\ 12 \\ 3\end{bmatrix}$$ $$3\vec{u}=3\begin{bmatrix}2 & 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}6 & 12 & 3\end{bmatrix}$$ And in ordered-set notation: $$3\vec{u}=3\langle2, 4, 1\rangle=\langle6, 12, 3\rangle$$Unlike with vector addition, scalar multiplication in cylindrical, spherical, or polar coordinates is very simple. Simply multiply the magnitude by the scalar value, and keep the direction constant. Some examples of scalar multiplication in other coordinate systems:

Spherical Coordinates
$$\vec{v}=4\boldsymbol{\hat{r}}+60°\boldsymbol{\hat{\theta}}+75°\boldsymbol{\hat{\phi}}$$ $$3\vec{v}=12\boldsymbol{\hat{r}}+60°\boldsymbol{\hat{\theta}}+75°\boldsymbol{\hat{\phi}}$$

Cylindrical Coordinates
$$\vec{w}=5\boldsymbol{\hat{\rho}}+15.4°\boldsymbol{\hat{\theta}}+2\boldsymbol{\hat{z}}$$ $$3\vec{w}=15\boldsymbol{\hat{\rho}}+15.4°\boldsymbol{\hat{\theta}}+6\boldsymbol{\hat{z}}$$

Polar Coordinates
$$\vec{x}=8\boldsymbol{\hat{r}}+52°\boldsymbol{\hat{\theta}}$$ $$3\vec{x}=24\boldsymbol{\hat{r}}+52°\boldsymbol{\hat{\theta}}$$
