Directions

This is a simulation of the wave functions of a free particle with energy states \( k \) and expansion coefficients \( C_{k} \) where \( k \) is any integer, positive, negative, or imaginary. To alter the energy state \( k \) and its corresponding expansion coefficient \( C_{k} \), click the input boxes on the left side of the simulation and change \( k \) to any positive or negative integer value, and \( C_{k} \) to any real, positive number. You can observe time evolution of the wave function by clicking "start/stop", and can reset the time value to zero at any time by clicking "reset t = 0". During time evolution of the wave function, you can click "measure P" to collapse the wave function and display the energy state chosen randomly, with probabilities proportional to the energy states' \( C_{k} \) value. You can populate the inputs section with between 20 and 200 input boxes with \( C_{k} \) values of 1 to examine the probability density function for a particle with equal expansion coefficients of every energy state. Press "reset defaults" at any point to reset the simulation to its default state.

Details
The wave function of energy state \( k \) is defined as:
$$ [1] \quad \psi_{k} = e^{ikx} $$
where \( x \) is the position of the particle in one dimension and \( \psi_{k} \) is the wave function of energy state \( k \). The wave function of the particle is a function of the wave functions of its' individual energy states, and their corresponding expansion coefficients.
$$ [2] \quad \Psi = \sum_{k = -\infty}^{\infty} C_{k} \psi_{k} $$
where \( C_{k} \) is the expansion coefficient of energy state \( k \), which corresponds to the likelihood of a particle being in that energy state. That is, a particle is most likely to be in an energy state with a large expansion coefficient. Time evolution of the wave function depends on the particle's mass \( m \):
$$ [3] \quad \psi_{k, t} = \sum_{k = -\infty}^{\infty} \psi_{k} e^{-i \hbar k^{2} t / 2 m} $$
where \( \hbar \) is the Planck constant and \( t \) is time. Thus, the definition of the time-dependent wave function of the particle is
$$ [4] \quad \Psi(t) = \sum_{j = -\infty}^{\infty} C_{k,j} \psi_{j} e^{-i \hbar k^{2} t / 2 m} $$
