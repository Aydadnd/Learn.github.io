Directions

A temperature-composition diagram is shown for two liquids (A, B) that are only partially miscible within the region enclosed by the orange and pink curve. Each phase in the two-phase region contains both A and B; the \(\alpha\) phase (represented by the pink line, mole fraction \(x_A^{\alpha}\)) is enriched in A and the \(\beta\) phase (orange line, \(x_A^{\beta}\)) is enriched in B. Outside the phase envelope, A and B are completely miscible. Sliders for temperature and overall mole fraction of A move the black dot around the diagram. The sizes of the rectangles at the top for pure A and pure B are proportional to the overall mole fraction of that component. The sizes of the containers on the right are proportional to the amounts of the phases (either \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) or a single miscible phase) in equilibrium, and the mole fractions are represented by the relative numbers of green (A) and blue (B) circles.

Details

The equilibrium data shown in a temperature-composition diagram is useful for determining the composition of each liquid phase after separating at different temperatures. This data is gathered empirically or with a thermodynamic model and represents the compositions at each temperature where the fugacities of each phase are equal. Using a mass balance, the mixing point composition of two liquids can be determined, and by drawing a horizontal line to each respective phase equilibrium curve, the composition of the respective equilibrium phases can be found. Using this information, a mass balance can be performed to find the relative amounts of liquid present in each phase: $$\text{In}-\text{Out}+\text{Generation}-\text{Consumption}=\text{Accumulation}$$ $$\text{In }=\text{ Out}$$ $$n^\text{(in)}=n^{\alpha}+n^{\beta}$$ $$x_A^\text{(in)} n^\text{(in)} = x_A^{\alpha}n^{\alpha} + x_A^{\beta}n^{\beta}$$ Where \(n^\text{(in)}\) represents the total amount of moles put into the system, \(n^\alpha\) and \(n^\beta\) represent the amount of moles in each phase, and \(x_A\) represents the mole fraction of A in each phase. This can be rearranged into the lever rule: $$ x_A^\text{(in)} n^I + x_A^\text{(in)} n^{II} = x_A^I n^I + x_A^{II} n^{II}$$ $$ (x_A^\text{(in)}-x_A^I)n^I=(x_A^{II}-x_A^\text{(in)})n^{II}$$ $$ n^I=\frac{(x_A^{II}-x_A^\text{(in)})}{(x_A^\text{(in)}-x_A^I)}n^{II}$$ Thus, by applying the lever rule between the points, \(x_A^\beta\), \(x_A^\alpha\), and x_A^\text{(in)}, the relative amounts of the alpha and beta phases can be calculated, as shown by the heights of the boxes in the simulation.
